Four generations up the same track

Eighty years of the same family, the same fields and the same stubborn belief that food tastes of how it was farmed.

Our story

Eleven cows, a borrowed horse, and no plan B

Frank and Edith Brindley took the tenancy on Bank House Farm in 1946 with eleven cows and a horse they had to give back. Their son George bought the farm outright in 1972, their granddaughter Alice runs the herd today, and her children are the fourth generation to learn to whistle the dogs from the yard gate.

The shop opened in 1998, in the old milking parlour, when milk prices fell below what it cost us to produce it. Rather than sell the herd, we started selling what the herd makes — first milk and eggs from a table by the gate, then the butchery, then everything you'll find under the tin roof now.

How we farm

Three rules that don't change

01

The herd comes first

Herefords and Lleyn sheep, bred for this hillside over decades, outdoors on grass for as much of the year as the weather allows. Calm animals, slow growing, handled by people they know. It shows on the plate.

02

Soil before yield

We rest fields, move the stock in rotation, muck the ground the old way and let the hedgerows grow thick. Not because it's fashionable — because Frank did it, and the grass has fed cattle for eighty years without wearing out.

03

Nothing wasted

Whole animals butchered, bones for stock, fat rendered for dripping, glut vegetables into the café's soup and the pantry's chutneys. If we can't sell it fresh, we cook it, cure it or preserve it — we don't bin it.

Milestones

Eighty years, five turning points

  1. 1946

    Frank and Edith take the tenancy

    Eleven cows, a borrowed horse and the first winter spent rebuilding walls the war years had let fall.

  2. 1972

    The family buys the farm

    George signs the papers on his fortieth birthday. The Hereford herd is established the same spring.

  3. 1998

    The shop opens in the old parlour

    Milk prices collapse; the family decides to sell what the herd makes rather than sell the herd. A table by the gate becomes a shop by autumn.

  4. 2011

    The butchery and cutting room

    Tom finishes his apprenticeship and comes home. For the first time, every animal is cut and sold from the farm it was born on.

  5. 2019

    Raw milk vending and rested fields

    The vending machine goes in by the yard gate, and the whole farm moves to rotational grazing — the fields get more rest, the grass gets deeper roots.
