In season

Why the asparagus season is six weeks — and why we won't stretch it

MB

Martha Brindley

The Shop & Café Corner · 12 May 2026 · 5 min read

Every May, usually in the first busy week, a visitor asks the same kind question at the counter: the asparagus is wonderful, so why don't we sell it all summer the way the supermarkets do? It deserves a proper answer, because the short season isn't a limitation we haven't solved. It's the whole point.

The bed in the walled garden went in the year the shop opened, so it has been feeding people for as long as the till has been ringing. Here's what those plants are actually doing — above ground and below it.

A spear is a stem that never got to be a fern

Asparagus is a perennial. The crowns under that bed are decades old, and every spear you eat is a young stem the plant sent up intending it to become a six-foot fern. When we cut spears, we're intercepting the plant's summer — politely, and only for a while.

The crown funds each spear from starch stored in its roots the previous year. Cut for six weeks and the crown can refill its larder once the ferns are allowed up. Cut all summer and you're spending the plant's savings without letting it earn:

  • Year one of over-cutting: the spears come thinner.
  • Year two: fewer spears, later start, and the weeds notice the gaps.
  • Year three: you're replanting the bed and waiting three more years for a first proper harvest.

Why supermarket asparagus doesn't stop

It isn't magic; it's aeroplanes. Out-of-season asparagus is cut in Peru or Mexico, chilled, flown, trucked and shelved — often a week or more from bed to basket. Asparagus starts converting its sugars within hours of cutting, which is why the flown stuff needs its woody ends snapping off and ours doesn't.

Ours is cut at dawn, on the counter by nine, and we'd rather sell nothing in August than sell that.

Getting the most out of six weeks

Rather than mourn a short season, plan for it:

  1. Eat it the day you buy it. Every hour costs sweetness. If you must keep it, stand it upright in a jar of water in the fridge like cut flowers.
  2. Don't overdress it. First-week spears want butter and nothing else. Save the hollandaise for week four, when you've stopped being amazed.
  3. Come on a Friday. Sourdough morning and peak asparagus overlap for about a fortnight. There are worse breakfasts than both, and the café corner serves exactly that.
  4. Mark the end. We stop cutting around the longest day, whatever's left in the bed. The last bundles get a shelf label saying so — they're the ones to buy two of.

After that the ferns go up, the bed disappears behind a green haze until the frosts, and the crowns get on with saving for next May. If you want a nod when the first spears break ground next spring, the monthly farm note always says so — or just watch for the chalkboard by the gate.
