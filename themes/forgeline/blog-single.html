The warehouse LED retrofit maths: payback in under 18 months

RO

Ray Okafor

Head of Lighting Design · 2 July 2026 · 8 min read

Every week someone sends us a quote from another supplier that shows a warehouse LED retrofit paying back in four years, and asks whether it's worth doing. It almost never is — at four years. But the four-year number is usually wrong, because it only counts the energy.

Done properly, a metal halide to LED conversion on a typical distribution unit pays back in 12 to 18 months. Here's the arithmetic we use, with nothing hidden.

Start with what the existing fittings really cost

A 400W metal halide high bay doesn't draw 400 watts. With control gear losses it's typically 425–450W at the meter. Run it 12 hours a day, six days a week, and at 24p/kWh each fitting costs around £405 a year in electricity alone.

Then add what the quotes leave out:

  • Relamping. Metal halide lamps fade fast and fail often. A planned two-year relamp on a 100-fitting shed — lamps, access equipment, labour — typically runs £3,500–£5,000 a cycle.
  • Lumen depreciation. By the end of lamp life you're getting 60% of the light for 100% of the energy. Most older sheds are under-lit and over-spending at the same time.
  • Access costs. Every spot failure above racking needs a powered-access visit. Two or three a year is normal, and £250 a visit adds up.

The replacement side

A 150W LED high bay at 140 lm/W replaces a 400W metal halide one-for-one in most layouts — and where it doesn't, a proper photometric layout fixes the spacing rather than adding fittings. The running cost falls to around £135 a year per fitting on the same duty cycle.

Per fitting, per year: £405 becomes £135. On a 100-fitting unit that's £27,000 a year before you count a single lamp change you no longer make.

A worked example

Real numbers from a 100-fitting distribution unit we supplied last quarter (names withheld, arithmetic intact):

  • Supply: 100 × 150W high bays at trade price — £8,450
  • Installation: like-for-like swap, existing circuits — £6,200
  • Annual energy saving — £27,000
  • Annual maintenance saving (relamp cycle + access visits, averaged) — £2,800

Total cost £14,650 against £29,800 of annual savings: payback in six months. That unit ran longer hours than most — at a more typical single-shift pattern the same sums land between 12 and 18 months. Either way it clears any sensible investment hurdle, and adding occupancy sensors in low-traffic aisles pulls it in further.

What to check before you sign anything

  1. Demand a photometric layout, not a fitting count. "One LED per metal halide" is a guess. A layout proves the lux levels and uniformity you'll actually get — and it's free from any supplier worth using.
  2. Check the efficacy at the fitting, not the chip. 140 lm/W luminaire efficacy is the honest number; chip-level figures flatter by 20% or more.
  3. Read the lifetime claim properly. L80B10 54,000 hours means something. "50,000 hour lifespan" with no L/B figures means nothing.
  4. Price the emergency variants at the same time. Retrofit is the cheapest moment you'll ever have to fix an emergency lighting shortfall — the access equipment is already on site.
